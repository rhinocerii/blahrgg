Lansing: Garza Presented and Passed an Ordinance That Favors His Union's Contractors

March 26, 2026

LANSING, Mich. — On August 25, 2025, the Lansing City Council passed Ordinance #1339, which changed how the city evaluates construction bids. Council Member Jeremy Garza, Vice President of UA Plumbers and Pipefitters Local 333, gave the public hearing overview on July 28, voted YEA on the 7-1 roll call, and moved to give the ordinance immediate effect. Three officers of his union testified in support. The sole NAY vote was Council Member Jackson.

What the Ordinance Does

Ordinance #1339 added Section 206.02(a)(1)I to the Lansing Codified Ordinances. It creates new "non-monetary criteria" for determining the "lowest and most responsive and responsible bidder" on city construction contracts. The ordinance opens with a statement of policy:

"When using public funds for construction work, especially those involving skilled trade workers, the City acknowledges that the best value and lowest overall cost is most often achieved through quality work done by a responsive and responsible bidder, rather than simply the lowest bidder."

Ordinance #1339, Section 206.02(a)(1)I, CivicClerk Event 6831.

The new criteria require construction bidders to document:

  • Participation in an apprenticeship program registered with the U.S. Department of Labor Office of Apprenticeship or a recognized State Apprenticeship Agency
  • An authorized OSHA 10-hour construction safety course for all workers on site
  • Employer-sponsored healthcare benefits
  • "Structured retirement plans, including pension or other employer provided plans"

Who Benefits

Union contractors maintain USDOL-registered apprenticeship programs. Union training centers provide OSHA certification. Union collective bargaining agreements include employer-sponsored healthcare and pension plans. Non-union contractors commonly use on-the-job training rather than registered apprenticeships, may not carry OSHA 10-hour certification for all field workers, and may offer 401(k) plans or no retirement benefit rather than pension plans.

The ordinance does not use the word "union." It does not need to. The criteria were written around the structure of union employment.

Who Testified

At the July 28 public hearing, three officers of UA Local 333 testified in support. Their roles and compensation are documented in the union's LM-2 annual report (DOL File 541-123, FY2025):

  • Dustin Howard — Business Manager. $161,452 total compensation. 20% of his time allocated to political activities and lobbying.
  • Jason Brown — Business Agent. $137,362 total compensation.
  • Deshon Leek — Executive Board member.

Non-union contractors who testified against the ordinance included Michael Marks, Tom Stark, and Jeremiah Leyba. At the August 25 vote, ten additional speakers supported the ordinance, including Howard again along with Aaron Pangborn, Ben Dodson, Anthony Medina, Elaina Plunkett, Jimmy Chapman, Derek Keesler, Jacob Toomey, Ken Kolp, and Tyler McCastle.

The Conflict

Garza is not a rank-and-file union member. He is Vice President of UA Local 333. The ordinance he presented, voted for, and gave immediate effect changes the city's construction bidding rules to favor the type of contractors that employ his union's members. Three of his union's officers organized testimony in support. The Local 333 PAC gave his campaign $24,500 on February 20, 2025, six months before this vote.

The Lansing City Charter (Section 5-505.3) states: "No elective officer, appointee or employee of the City may participate in, vote upon or act upon any matter if a conflict exists." Garza did not recuse. A review of 52 City Council meeting minutes from January 2024 through March 2026 found no evidence of Garza recusing from any development, construction, or building trades-related vote.

Six Weeks Later

On October 13, 2025, the Council passed Ordinance #1343, raising the competitive bidding threshold from $15,000 to $31,000 (7-0, with Jackson absent; corrected October 27, 7-1). Garza again moved immediate effect. Contracts under $31,000 no longer require formal competitive sealed bids. Combined with Ordinance #1339's criteria favoring union-qualified bidders, the effect is that mid-range construction contracts can be awarded without competition, and larger contracts are evaluated on criteria that match union shop qualifications.
