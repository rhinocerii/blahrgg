Lansing: What Residents Can Do Before the Deep Green Vote

March 26, 2026

LANSING, Mich. — The Deep Green land sale requires six of eight council votes. The rezoning hearing is set for April 6. Michigan law and the Lansing City Charter give residents several tools that can directly affect the outcome. Most of them have deadlines. Here is how each one works.

Protest Petition — Force a Supermajority on the Rezoning

The Deep Green rezoning (Z-3-2026, from DT-3 Urban Core to IND-1 Industrial) is an ordinance that currently requires a simple majority of 5 votes to pass. A protest petition raises the threshold to two-thirds — 6 of 8.

Under the Michigan Zoning Enabling Act (MCL 125.3403):

"If a protest petition is filed, approval of the amendment to the zoning ordinance shall require a 2/3 vote of the legislative body."

The petition must be signed by owners of at least 20% of the land area within 100 feet of the boundary of the rezoning parcels (Kalamazoo/Cedar/Larch, Parcel IDs 33-01-01-16-427-122, 33-01-01-16-427-082, 33-01-01-16-427-051, 33-01-01-16-427-192). Publicly owned land is excluded from the 20% calculation.

The petition must be presented to the City Council before the final vote on the rezoning. No specific form is required. A letter stating opposition to Z-3-2026 with property owner signatures and parcel IDs is sufficient.

The land sale already requires 6 votes under Charter Section 8-403.4. The protest petition closes the gap on the rezoning, making both votes require the same supermajority.

Board of Ethics Opinion — Independent Review

Any person can request that the Board of Ethics (Charter Section 5-503.2) render a formal opinion "on its own initiative or upon request" on any matter within its authority. The Board can also find a prior action ethically improper and advise reconsideration (Section 5-503.3).

In 2025, the Board of Ethics rejected the City Attorney's determination that Council Member Tamera Carter had no conflict on a property vote involving her sister-in-law. The Board has demonstrated that it acts independently of the administration.

Possible subjects for a Board opinion:

  • Whether Council Member Jeremy Garza, Vice President of UA Plumbers and Pipefitters Local 333, has a conflict of interest on a project with explicit union labor commitments
  • Whether a PR consultant simultaneously serving on the PAC committee that endorsed the council majority creates a conflict requiring disclosure

Ethics Complaints — Create a Public Record

The City Charter (Section 5-505.1) requires city officers who "may derive any income or benefit, directly or indirectly, from a contract with the City or from any City action" to file an affidavit with the City Clerk at least ten days before the action. Section 5-505.3 states: "No elective officer, appointee or employee of the City may participate in, vote upon or act upon any matter if a conflict exists."

An ethics complaint filed with the Board of Ethics through the City Clerk creates a public record of the conflict question, forces the named official to respond, and establishes grounds for a legal challenge if a conflicted member votes.

Conflict Challenge on the Council Floor

Under Charter Section 3-205.2, any Council member can raise a conflict of interest question before the main vote. The remaining members (excluding the member in question) vote on whether the conflict exists. If found, the member cannot vote.

A resident cannot raise this directly during a vote but can request it through public comment, putting the charter language and the specific facts on the record so that a council member can act on it.

Referendum Petition — Post-Vote Backstop

If the rezoning ordinance passes, Charter Section 2-404 allows residents to file a referendum petition within 30 days. If the petition contains signatures from 5% of registered City electors, the ordinance is suspended until the voters decide. Lansing has 80,415 active registered voters. Five percent is 4,021 signatures.

The rezoning (Z-3-2026) is an ordinance and is subject to referendum. The land sale (Act 7-2025) is a resolution, which may not be. But the project cannot proceed without industrial zoning. A successful rezoning referendum would prevent the data center regardless of the land sale outcome.

Court Petition for Forfeiture Hearing

If a Council member with a conflict votes and the Council does not act, Charter Section 2-302.3 allows any resident to petition a court to require the Council to hold a public hearing on the forfeiture of an office. Violations of the conflict provisions "may be made punishable by forfeiture of office or position" (Section 5-504.3).

Public Comment

Everything above is strengthened by what gets said on the record at council meetings. Public comment is broadcast on City TV, entered into the minutes, and becomes part of the permanent record. Specific facts cited during public comment — charter provisions, campaign finance figures, conflict documentation — create a record that council members must address or ignore publicly.

The March 30 Committee of the Whole Special Meeting and the April 6 hearing are the remaining opportunities before the vote.
