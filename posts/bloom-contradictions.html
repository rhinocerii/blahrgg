Lansing: What Bloom Energy's VP Said at the Hearing vs. What the Record Shows

March 26, 2026

LANSING, Mich. — Bloom Energy VP Marisa Blackshire flew in from San Jose to address the Lansing City Council on March 23. She described the company's fuel cells as clean, quiet, safe, and well-regulated. Bloom's own filings and enforcement history describe something else.

Bloom Energy (NYSE: BE) will supply and operate the 16MW solid oxide fuel cell facility that provides two-thirds of the Deep Green data center's power. The company's identity was not disclosed to the public until January 2026, three months after the project was announced. Its role as the facility's operator was not revealed until approximately March 2026.

On Safety and Certification

What Blackshire said: "The systems themselves are certified by UL laboratories. They have to meet UL's safety standards. In addition to that, our installations have to meet fire code, building code, electrical code."

What the record shows: In 2016, Bloom Energy was sued by Unicat Catalyst Technologies for allegedly dumping toxic benzene into the community's wastewater in Sunnyvale, California. Separately, the EPA designated Bloom as a Large Quantity Generator of hazardous waste, including benzene, chromium, and lead. The EPA levied a $1.37 million fine for hazardous waste mishandling. UL certification covers the equipment's design. It does not address how the company handles the waste products those systems generate.

On Emissions

What Deep Green CTO Matt Craggs said: Fuel cells "reduce sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxide emissions by over 99.5% and carbon dioxide emissions by nearly 50%" compared to the MISO regional grid.

What the record shows: After degradation, Bloom fuel cells emit over 825 pounds of CO2 per megawatt-hour, according to analysis of Bloom's own performance data. A combined-cycle natural gas plant emits 896 lbs/MWh. The gap is less than 8%. Ninety-one percent of Bloom fuel cells worldwide run on natural gas. The "50% reduction" compares fuel cells to the dirtiest marginal generation on the MISO grid, not to the grid average or to the status quo without a data center.

A University of Delaware study found that Bloom overstated the efficiency of its fuel cells by up to 20%.

On Reliability and Lifespan

What was implied: The fuel cell facility would operate reliably for the duration of the 20-year BWL contract.

What the record shows: Hindenburg Research (September 2019) documented that post-2017 Bloom fuel cell stacks had a lifespan under three years. Bloom's Delaware servers were decommissioned after approximately seven years, against an expected lifespan of 15 to 21 years. Bloom restated its financials for the 18-month period surrounding its IPO, acknowledging "material misstatements" in its SEC filings.

On the Delaware Track Record

What was not mentioned: Bloom received over $580 million in public subsidies in Delaware. The company promised 1,500 jobs and delivered under 800. It promised six campus buildings and built zero. Delaware State Senator Harris McDowell told reporters: "I think we were sold a bill of goods."

Deep Green CTO Craggs, asked by Council Member Kost what the incentive was for the project, compared the data center industry to the tobacco industry — arguing that Deep Green needs to prove it can be different to avoid losing its "public licence to operate." He did not mention Bloom's Delaware record.

On the Operator Role

What Blackshire said: Bloom is an "equipment manufacturer that deploys on-site power solutions for customers" and will "operate and maintain the Bloom equipment in partnership with and at the direction of BWL."

What the public was told at announcement: On November 5, 2025, BWL's press release stated BWL would provide all power. No mention of an on-site power plant, fuel cells, or a third-party operator. On November 6, BWL General Manager Dick Peffley confirmed fuel cells existed but declined to name the company, citing an NDA. Bloom's identity was revealed in January 2026. Its operator role was not disclosed until approximately March 2026.

The three-party liability chain — Deep Green builds, BWL owns, Bloom operates — is more complex than Bloom's typical installations. The Bloom service contract has not been disclosed to the public.

On Desulfurization Waste

What Blackshire said in her March 22 letter to Council: Desulfurization canisters are "shipped to a recycler in Indiana" and classified as "Excluded Recyclable Material," not hazardous waste.

What the record shows: The desulfurization process removes sulfur from the natural gas feed. The canisters contain copper catalyst material and are replaced every 15 to 36 months. Bloom's EPA designation as a Large Quantity Generator of hazardous waste applies to its manufacturing facilities. Whether the Lansing installation's waste stream would carry the same designation depends on the specific chemicals and quantities generated at this site. No site-specific waste characterization has been presented to the council.
