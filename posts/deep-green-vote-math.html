Lansing: A Conflict Question Could Reshape the Deep Green Land Sale Vote

March 24, 2026

LANSING, Mich. — The Lansing City Charter requires a two-thirds supermajority to sell city-owned real property. On an eight-member Council, that means six yes votes. WLNS reported in mid-March that Deep Green's $1.4 million land purchase had five likely supporters. Two Council members have publicly indicated they would vote no. A third told WLNS she was "leaning no."

Among the five expected yes votes is Council Member Jeremy Garza, Vice President of UA Plumbers and Pipefitters Local 333. Deep Green has committed to using union construction labor for the project, and a Local 333 member told the March 23 Council meeting he anticipated a 20-year maintenance contract. The Local 333 PAC has given Garza $24,500 across his Council campaigns.

The Charter prohibits Council members from voting on questions where they hold a financial interest. If Garza were found to have one and could not vote, the project's maximum possible yes count would drop to five, and the sale would not pass.

The Charter Provisions

Section 8-403.4 of the Lansing City Charter, under "Purchase and Sale of Real Property":

"No interest in real property may be sold by the City without either the affirmative vote of the people or the affirmative vote of two-thirds (2/3) of the Council members serving."

Lansing City Charter, Section 8-403.4, charter page 50.

Section 3-205.1 defines how the two-thirds threshold is calculated:

"A vote of two-thirds (2/3) of the Council shall require an affirmative vote of two-thirds (2/3) of the number of members elected. If there are one or more vacancies existing on the Council and a vote of two-thirds (2/3) of the Council members serving is required, an affirmative vote of two-thirds (2/3) of the Council members serving shall be sufficient to adopt."

Lansing City Charter, Section 3-205.1, charter page 13.

Eight Council members were elected. Five yes votes out of eight is 62.5%, which falls short of two-thirds. Six out of eight is 75%, which clears it. The minimum to approve a property sale is six.

The Charter includes one exception: if there is a vacancy (an unfilled seat), the denominator shifts to "members serving." A vacancy is defined in Section 2-303 of the Charter. All eight seats on the current Council are filled.

The Conflict Question

Section 3-205.2 of the Charter addresses conflicts of interest:

"Each member of the Council shall vote on each question before the Council for a determination, unless excused there from by the affirmative vote of two-thirds (2/3) of the members serving, except that no member shall vote on any question upon which that member has a conflict of interest or a financial interest other than as a citizen of the City. If a conflict of interest question is raised under this section at any Council meeting, such question shall be determined by a majority of those Council members present and qualified to vote before the main question shall be voted on, but the Council member affected shall not vote on such determination."

Lansing City Charter, Section 3-205.2, charter pages 13-14.

UA Local 333's leadership page lists Garza as Vice President, not a rank-and-file member. The union represents plumbers and pipefitters across mid-Michigan.

At the March 23 Council meeting, Deep Green CTO Matt Craggs said the company is "committed to using union construction jobs" and is "working towards an agreement with Union Labor." Derek Wright, identifying himself as a member of UA Plumbers and Pipe Fitters Local 333, testified in support: "I support the deep green primarily for jobs for my local brothers and sisters and myself. And then if there's going to be a 20-year maintenance contract, that will also provide money and payment and taxes from my local brothers and sisters."

Whether Garza's role as union VP constitutes a "financial interest other than as a citizen of the City" under Section 3-205.2 is a determination the Council would make before the vote. The Charter says a majority of qualified members present would decide. Garza would not participate in that determination.

The Math

A recusal would not trigger the vacancy exception. Garza's seat would not be empty; he would remain a serving member of Council, prohibited from voting on one question. The denominator stays at eight. The threshold stays at six.

With Garza out, seven members would vote. Council Members Ryan Kost and Adam Hussain have both indicated they would vote no (WLNS), leaving a maximum of five yes votes.

Precedent

Lansing has recent experience with recusals on property votes. In 2025, At-Large Council Member Tamera Carter recused herself twice from votes involving a property owned by her sister-in-law. The Council approved those recusals. Later, Carter voted on the same property after City Attorney Gregory Venker told her there was no conflict. WLNS reported that City Clerk Chris Swope admitted the recusal motions were not recorded in the minutes or forwarded to the Board of Ethics, as required by the Ethics Ordinance. The Board of Ethics rejected the City Attorney's determination that no conflict existed.

City Attorney Venker would also be the official consulted if a conflict question were raised about Garza's vote on the Deep Green land sale. Clerk Swope would be responsible for recording any recusal under the Ethics Ordinance.

How a Conflict Question Gets Raised

The Charter provides more than one path.

Under Section 3-205.2, a conflict question can be raised at any Council meeting and decided by a majority of members present. Any Council member can raise it before the main question is voted on.

Under Section 5-505.1, a city officer who "may derive any income or benefit, directly or indirectly, from a contract with the City or from any City action" is required to file an affidavit with the City Clerk at least ten days before the action. The Clerk is then required to notify City Council at the next regular meeting. Under Section 5-505.3, "no elective officer, appointee or employee of the City may participate in, vote upon or act upon any matter if a conflict exists."

The Board of Ethics (Section 5-503.2) may render a formal opinion "on its own initiative or upon request" on any matter within its authority. A resident can request that opinion. The Board can also find a prior action ethically improper and advise that it be reconsidered (Section 5-503.3). In the Carter precedent, the Board of Ethics overruled the City Attorney's determination that no conflict existed.

Violations of the conflict of interest provisions are punishable "to the maximum extent permitted by law and may be made punishable by forfeiture of office or position" (Section 5-504.3).

Current Status

The land sale hearing was held March 23, 2026. The rezoning hearing is set for April 6. The land sale was referred to the Committee of the Whole. No vote has been taken on either the sale or the rezoning.

Forty-three members of the public spoke against the project at the March 23 hearing. Six spoke in favor. The public comment count comes from a full review of the meeting transcript.
