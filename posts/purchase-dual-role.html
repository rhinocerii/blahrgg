Lansing: A Union Comms Director Testified for Deep Green as a Fire Commissioner

March 26, 2026

LANSING, Mich. — At the March 23 City Council meeting, Steve Purchase introduced himself as a member of the Board of Fire Commissioners speaking "in my personal capacity as a 20-year resident." He testified in favor of selling city-owned parking lots to Deep Green Technologies, connecting the vote directly to fire department funding. He did not mention his day job.

Purchase is the Communications Director for the Michigan Regional Council of Carpenters, a building trades union whose members benefit from construction projects. He chairs the Board of Fire Commissioners, a mayoral appointment. He sits on the board of Michigan Climate Jobs, a union-labor coalition that launched on February 10, 2026 — the same day as the first Deep Green City Council hearing. He was endorsed for the Lansing Charter Commission by the same labor-business coalition that ran Vote Yes Lansing 2025.

What Purchase Said

"I see fire department budgets every year. And year after year, we hear about the need for a flat budget. Meanwhile, over the last decade, our call volume has increased by 35%, 7,000 calls a year. That's over 20 calls every single day. And we desperately need to invest to increase our personnel to support our medic units that are among the busiest in the country and the only way to do that without asking all of these fine folks for a tax increase is to increase our tax base and that's what this vote can do."

Steve Purchase, March 23, 2026 City Council meeting.

The message: vote yes on the data center or the fire department suffers. A union communications professional delivered it while wearing the credibility of a fire commission appointment.

What the Mayor Said First

Earlier the same evening, Mayor Andy Schor proposed a supplemental budget tying $1 million per year in Deep Green BWL return-on-equity revenue to specific programs. Of that, $400,000 would go to the fire department for "specialized gear for electrical hazard mitigation, chemical spill response, confined space rescue, high angle rescue certifications, electrical hazard, HAZMAT, and additional personnel."

These are not general staffing needs. They are the specific capabilities required to respond to incidents at a facility with fuel cells, glycol cooling systems, and high-voltage electrical infrastructure. Council Member Deyanira Nevarez Martinez asked the mayor directly whether the HAZMAT funding was "in preparation because we may not have the HAZMAT available needed in case this blew up or there was like a you know a leak or something." The mayor's response: "It was, well, it was general."

The mayor said the IAFF, the International Association of Fire Fighters, recommended the allocations.

What Is Not in the Budget

The $400,000 fire/HAZMAT allocation does not appear in the FY27 Budget Resolution. It was not submitted as a written amendment. It was not referred to the Committee on Public Safety. It exists only as a verbal proposal made at the same meeting as the Deep Green public hearing.

The base FY27 budget allocates $48,755,900 to the fire department ($40.9 million personnel, $7.8 million operating). The "Council Changes" column is empty. The HAZMAT allocation is a promise, not an appropriation.

What the Fire Department Actually Needs

The fire department's staffing crisis is real. WKAR reported in April 2025 that daily staffing dropped from 52 to 41 firefighters since 2011 while call volume increased 60%. Fire Chief Brian Sturdivant called the proposed 1.2% personnel increase insufficient. The department has two ambulances pending state approval and needs approximately 12 to match proportional coverage.

The question is not whether the fire department needs funding. It does. The question is why $400,000 in HAZMAT capability was made contingent on a data center vote instead of being included in the base budget. The Ovation Center, Tower on Grand, Public Safety Complex, and new City Hall are all under construction or approved. If Lansing needs HAZMAT response capability for "buildings going vertical," as the mayor said, that need exists regardless of whether Deep Green is approved.

The Michigan Climate Jobs Timing

Michigan Climate Jobs, the union coalition Purchase sits on, launched on February 10, 2026. The first Deep Green City Council hearing was on February 9, 2026. The coalition's member organizations include the Michigan Pipe Trades Association (parent of UA Local 333), the Northern Midwest Regional Council of Carpenters (Purchase's employer), Operating Engineers 324, and IBEW. Its chair is Ryan Bennet, president of the Michigan Pipe Trades Association.

The IAFF and the Mayor

IAFF Local 421 pulled its endorsement of Mayor Schor in June 2025 over retiree healthcare costs. Schor negotiated a deal and the union re-endorsed him in September 2025. IAFF president Brad Jorae said the mayor "assured us of his commitment to addressing this issue and has fulfilled that promise." The supplemental budget fire allocation came six months later. The IAFF has demonstrated it uses endorsement leverage. The mayor has demonstrated he makes concessions to maintain it.
